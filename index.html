Software Estimation Techniques:

1. Top-Down Method (Macro Model):

Approach: Starts with an overall project cost and breaks it into components.

Advantages: Focuses on often overlooked system-level activities, requires minimal project detail.

Disadvantages: May miss complex low-level problems, lacks detailed justification.

2. Bottom-Up Method (COCOMO's detailed model.):

Approach: Estimates costs of individual components and combines them for the project cost.

Advantages: Allows traditional estimation, offers more stable estimates.

Disadvantages: May overlook system-level costs, can be inaccurate in early phases, may be time-consuming, and may not be feasible with limited resources.

COCOMO (COnstructive COst MOdel)

COCOMO is a software cost estimation model developed by Barry Boehm in the late 1970s, providing estimates for effort, time, and cost in software projects.

Versions:

  1. Basic COCOMO: Simple estimate based on project size (Organic, Semi-detached, Embedded).

    Equations: Effort = a1 x (KLOC)^a2 PM, Tdev = b1 x (Effort)^b2 Months.

    Parameters: KLOC, a1, a2, b1, b2, Tdev, Effort.

  1. Intermediate COCOMO: Considers project complexity, team capability, and flexibility.
  1. Detailed COCOMO: More sophisticated, includes factors like lines of code, development environment, team experience.

Estimation Process:

  1. SLOC Estimation: Determine the size of the software in lines of code.
  1. Mode Selection: Choose mode based on project nature (Organic, Semi-detached, Embedded).
  1. Adjustment Factors: Consider various factors to adjust the basic effort estimation.
  1. Effort and Duration Calculation: Calculate final effort and duration estimates based on adjusted parameters.

Usage:
COCOMO helps project managers estimate resource requirements early in the project but provides approximations, subject to unforeseen factors. Other estimation models and agile practices are also commonly used in conjunction with or as alternatives to COCOMO.

Monte Carlo Analysis in Project Management

Quantitative risk analysis technique. Invented by Stanislaw Ulam in 1940.

Mathematical simulation for decision-making. Evaluates impact through multiple simulations.

Helps in uncertain decision scenarios. Prevents surprises in later stages.

Applied for risk analysis in projects. Provides outcome range and probabilities.

Example:

Consider three activities with PERT estimates suggesting a 17.5-month completion time. Best case: 16 months, worst case: 21 months.

After running Monte Carlo simulation 500 times, potential completion percentages are as follows:

  • 2% chance in 16 months
  • 8% chance in 17 months
  • 55% chance in 18 months
  • 70% chance in 19 months
  • 95% chance in 20 months
  • 100% chance in 21 months

Monte Carlo provides detailed data analysis for more informed decision-making.

Limitations of Monte Carlo Analysis:

  • Results depend on estimate quality; biased data gives false results.
  • Shows task completion probability, not actual time needed.
  • Not useful for single activities; requires multiple with risk assessments.
  • Requires additional software for simulation.

Earned Value Analysis (EVA): in project management is a robust tool for tracking costs and timelines. Key metrics include:

  1. Planned Value (PV): Budgeted labor costs for planned project activities.

    Formula: Planned Value = (Planned % Complete) × (Budget at Completion)

  1. Actual Cost (AC): Actual labor costs for completed project activities.
  1. Remaining Cost (RC): Forecasted labor costs for remaining activities.

    Percent spent = AC / (AC + RC).

  1. Earned Value (EV): Calculated by multiplying Planned Value by Percent Spent.

Product quality and Process quality - (Metrics idk man)

Product quality and process quality are two distinct but interconnected aspects of quality management in any industry.

Product Quality:

Refers to the characteristics and attributes of a final output, such as a product or service, that determine its ability to meet specified requirements and satisfy customer needs.

  1. Conformance to Requirements: Degree of adherence to specified standards and requirements.
  1. Fitness for Use: Ability of the product to satisfy user needs and expectations.
  1. Reliability: Consistency and dependability of the product's performance.
  1. Durability: Product's ability to withstand wear and tear over time.
  1. Performance: Efficiency, speed, and responsiveness of the product.
  1. Safety and Compliance: Adherence to safety standards and regulatory requirements.

Process Quality:

Relates to the effectiveness and efficiency of the methods, procedures, and workflows used to create a product or deliver a service.

  1. Consistency: Repeatability of processes for consistent outcomes.
  1. Efficiency: Achievement of objectives with minimal resource waste.
  1. Effectiveness: Alignment of processes with intended goals for overall success.
  1. Adaptability: Capacity of processes to respond to changing conditions.
  1. Continuous Improvement: Commitment to ongoing enhancement of processes.
  1. Documentation and Standardization: Clear documentation and adherence to established standards.

Statistical Process Control (SPC)

A quality control method that employs statistical techniques to monitor, control, and improve processes. It involves using statistical tools to analyze and manage variations in a process to ensure consistent and high-quality output.

Why We Use It: To maintain and enhance the quality of processes by identifying and addressing variations. It helps organizations achieve better control over their processes, leading to increased efficiency, reduced defects, and improved overall product or service quality.

Use of SPC:

  1. Data Collection: Systematic data gathering.
  1. Control Charts: Visualize process variations.
  1. Analyzing Data: Statistical analysis for informed decisions.

Applications:

Manufacturing: Controlling and improving production processes.

Healthcare: Monitoring and enhancing healthcare delivery processes.

Service Industries: Ensuring consistency in service-oriented processes.

Software Development, Supply Chain Mangement, Environmental Monitoring

Features:

Real-Time Monitoring: Provides real-time insights into process performance.

Preventive Action: Allows for proactive identification and correction of potential issues.

Continuous Improvement: Facilitates ongoing improvement by addressing variations systematically.

Decision Support: Assists in data-driven decision-making for process adjustments.
